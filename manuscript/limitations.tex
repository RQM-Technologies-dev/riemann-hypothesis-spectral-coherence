This section is essential. It records, candidly and precisely, the limitations
of the current manuscript and the steps that have not yet been rigorously established.
Readers should treat this section as the most important part of the manuscript
for the purpose of mathematical audit.

\subsection{Geometric Language Is Not a Proof}

The framework uses geometric and physical language throughout: ``balance axis,''
``cancellation state,'' ``spectral coherence,'' ``reflection,'' ``destructive
interference.'' This language is useful for communicating intuitions and for
organizing the structure of the argument. It is not, by itself, a mathematical
proof.

In particular:
\begin{itemize}
  \item A geometric picture of zeros ``balancing'' on the critical line is not an
        argument that zeros cannot exist elsewhere.
  \item An analogy between spectral cancellation and a standing wave does not
        establish any property of $\zeta$ or of the proposed operator $H$.
  \item The intuition that symmetry ``forces'' balance points to the symmetry axis
        is a claim that must be proved, not a fact that follows from the symmetry alone.
\end{itemize}

Every informal statement in this manuscript must ultimately be replaced by a precise
definition or a proved theorem before the argument can be accepted as a proof.

\subsection{The Operator Construction Must Be Made Rigorous}

The spectral coherence operator $H$ (Definition~\ref{def:H}) is not yet fully
defined. The manuscript describes the intended properties of $H$ — its symmetry,
its proposed spectral relationship to $\zeta$, its balance criterion — but does
not provide a complete mathematical construction.

Until $H$ is defined as a genuine operator on a specified Hilbert or Banach space,
with a domain, a clear spectral theory, and proved properties, the argument
cannot proceed past the motivational stage. This is the most urgent open task.

\subsection{The Correspondence Between Balance and Zeros Must Be Proved}

Even if $H$ is fully defined, it must be proved — not assumed — that the nontrivial
zeros of $\zeta$ correspond exactly to the balance points of $H$
(Conjecture~\ref{conj:correspondence}). This correspondence is the analytical
engine of the argument.

The proof of this correspondence is likely to require:
\begin{itemize}
  \item An explicit relationship between the spectral measure or resolvent of $H$
        and the function $\xi$ or $\zeta$.
  \item A calculation showing that the balance criterion characterizes the zero
        set of $\zeta$ in the critical strip.
  \item Verification that the correspondence is not merely approximate or
        asymptotic.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Uniqueness of the Balance Axis Must Be Proved}

The claim that the balance criterion is satisfiable only on $\Re(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$
(Conjecture~\ref{conj:uniqueness}) is the central proposed contribution of the
framework. It is also the primary open gap.

This claim cannot be established by symmetry alone (see \S3). It requires a
positive argument showing that the balance condition fails for all $s$ with
$\Re(s) \ne \tfrac{1}{2}$. The current manuscript does not contain such an argument;
it contains only the motivation and the proposed logical structure.

\subsection{All Off-Critical-Line Possibilities Must Be Handled}

A proof of RH must rule out all off-line zeros, not just ``generic'' or
``typical'' ones. This includes:
\begin{itemize}
  \item Zeros at large $|\Im(s)|$, where asymptotic methods may be needed.
  \item Zeros near the edges of the critical strip (near $\Re(s) = 0$ or $\Re(s) = 1$),
        where boundary behavior may differ.
  \item Any exceptional or pathological configurations that the framework might not
        address uniformly.
\end{itemize}

The proposed framework does not currently address these cases individually. A
complete argument must either treat all cases uniformly or provide separate
arguments for each exceptional class.

\subsection{Dependence on Analogy Must Be Eliminated}

As noted above, several steps in the current argument rely on analogies (between
balance and standing waves, between spectral coherence and interference, etc.).
These analogies are heuristically valuable but mathematically insufficient.

A completed proof must contain no steps that rest solely on analogy. Every
logical inference must follow from a definition or a previously proved lemma.
Gaps where analogy currently substitutes for proof are identified in the proof
architecture (\S7) and in the notes file \texttt{notes/known-gaps.md}.
