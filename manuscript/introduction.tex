The Riemann Hypothesis (RH) asserts that all nontrivial zeros of the Riemann zeta
function $\zeta(s)$ lie on the critical line $\Re(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$. First stated
by Bernhard Riemann in 1859~\cite{Riemann1859}, the hypothesis remains one of the
central open problems in mathematics. Extensive numerical verification has confirmed
the hypothesis for the first $10^{13}$ zeros~\cite{Odlyzko1987, PlattTrudgian2021},
but no proof is known.

The difficulty of RH lies not in the complexity of its statement but in the absence
of a mechanism that would explain \emph{why} all zeros are confined to the critical
line. A proof requires more than numerical evidence; it requires a structural
argument showing that off-line zeros are impossible.

This manuscript attempts to formalize a spectral-coherence interpretation of the
Riemann Hypothesis. The central thesis is that the nontrivial zeros of $\zeta$ are
not merely observed to lie on the critical line but are \emph{forced} there by a
balance condition arising from the reflection symmetry of the completed zeta function
$\xi(s) = \xi(1-s)$.

The approach developed here treats nontrivial zeros as balance points of a spectral
object $H$ associated with $\xi$. The reflection symmetry $s \mapsto 1-s$ acts on
the spectrum of $H$, and the claim is that balance states — states at which a
prescribed cancellation condition holds — can only occur at the fixed point of this
reflection, namely the critical line $\Re(s) = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

The burden of proof lies in converting this interpretation into a rigorous
operator-theoretic or analytic argument. The current manuscript does not claim to
have completed this task. Rather, it develops the conceptual framework, isolates
the key steps, and identifies the open gaps with precision. The argument is
presented as a structured proof program for external mathematical review.
